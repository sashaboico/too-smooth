# TooSmooth Labeling Guide

This guide defines the 3-class schema used to label messages in `data/labeled/`.
Consistency between labelers matters more than any single call — when in doubt,
follow the decision tree and record your reasoning in the sample's `notes` field.

The six features selected for extraction align with manipulation patterns identified
in peer-reviewed literature on human factors in AI-driven phishing (Jabir et al., 2025),
specifically: urgency exploitation, authority impersonation, emotional pressure, and
hyper-personalization as the primary attack vectors GenAI amplifies over traditional phishing.

## The core idea

Every label answers two questions, **in this order**:

1. **Intent** — is this an attack (deceptive social-engineering intent)?
2. **Authorship** — if it's an attack, was it written by a human or generated by an LLM?

Never collapse these. Smoothness is an *authorship* signal and must never decide the
intent question; intent is about what the message is *trying to do*, not how polished
it reads. A flawless, well-structured message can be perfectly legitimate, and a
typo-ridden one can be a genuine scam.

## Where the labels come from

Our base corpus (`data/raw/`, see [SOURCE.md](../data/raw/SOURCE.md)) ships only **two**
of the three classes — `label 0` → `legitimate`, `label 1` → `human_phishing`. The
**`ai_phishing`** class does not exist in any public corpus and must be sourced or
generated (LLM-produced phishing). That makes the human-vs-AI authorship distinction the
part of this schema most exposed to labeling error — treat it with the most care.

## Class Definitions

### `legitimate`
A genuine message with no social-engineering intent. The sender is who they claim to be,
the request is real and appropriate, and any urgency / authority / emotion is
proportionate to a real situation.

Criteria (all should hold):
- No attempt to impersonate a person, brand, or institution.
- The call-to-action (link, credential entry, payment, reply) is legitimate and expected
  in context.
- Pressure cues, if present, reflect a real deadline (a genuine invoice due date, a real
  sale), not a manufactured threat ("your account will be suspended").

Positive indicators: verifiable sender domain, expected context, no credential/payment
lure, links that match the purported sender.

### `human_phishing`
A social-engineering attack written by a human. Malicious intent is present, and the
*execution* shows human authorship.

Criteria (attack intent **and** human-authorship tells):
- Clear manipulation: spoofed authority, manufactured urgency, credential/payment lure,
  deceptive links, etc.
- Human-authorship tells: spelling/grammar errors, awkward or non-native phrasing,
  inconsistent register or tone shifts, formatting glitches, copy-paste seams, a flat
  "straight to the ask" structure with no real arc.

Reference exemplars in our corpus: `Nazario.csv`, `Nigerian_Fraud.csv` — classic
human-written phishing and 419 scams.

### `ai_phishing`
A social-engineering attack generated (wholly or mostly) by an LLM. Same malicious intent
as `human_phishing`, but the execution is "too smooth."

Criteria (attack intent **and** AI-authorship tells):
- Clear manipulation, as above.
- AI-authorship tells: near-perfect fluency, even tone and rhythm throughout, a complete
  and well-formed persuasion arc (rapport → justification → pressure → ask), institutional
  register reproduced flawlessly, deep personalization at apparent scale, and the *absence*
  of the human error artifacts above.

> `ai_phishing` is a judgment about **authorship**, not severity. An AI-written attack is
> not inherently more dangerous — but separating the two tells us *how* attacks are being
> produced and lets the classifier learn the smoothness signature that is TooSmooth's whole
> premise.

## Edge Cases

1. **Legitimate-but-clumsy corporate mail.** A real HR notice full of typos and urgent
   "action required" language. Intent is genuine → **`legitimate`**. Errors and urgency are
   *not* sufficient for an attack label; intent governs the first split.

2. **AI-polished human scam (mixed authorship).** A human scammer ran their draft through
   an LLM to fix grammar. Label by the *dominant* signal in the final text: uniformly fluent
   with a clean arc → **`ai_phishing`**; human structure/seams survive the polish →
   **`human_phishing`**. Record the ambiguity in `notes`.

3. **Legitimate message written with AI assistance.** A real colleague used an LLM to draft
   a genuine request. Smooth and well-structured, but no malicious intent → **`legitimate`**.
   Smoothness alone never implies attack; the first split is always intent. As benign senders
   increasingly use AI, this case grows — guard against it explicitly.

4. **Benign mass marketing with urgency.** "Sale ends tonight!" from a retailer you opted
   into. A real (if aggressive) commercial deadline, no impersonation → **`legitimate`**.
   Distinguish *manufactured-threat* urgency ("account suspended") from *commercial* urgency.

5. **Suspicious but unverifiable intent.** Looks like an attack but you cannot confirm intent
   from the text alone (e.g. an internal-looking payment-change request). Treat plausible
   impersonation + an unusual financial/credential ask as an attack, then split on authorship.
   If genuinely undecidable, escalate to a second labeler — do not guess.

6. **AI text with deliberately injected errors (evasion).** An attacker prompts the model to
   add typos to dodge "too smooth" detection. If you can spot tells that the errors are
   *superficial* over an otherwise uniform, well-arced body (consistent advanced vocabulary,
   perfect punctuation elsewhere, intact persuasion structure) → lean **`ai_phishing`** and
   flag in `notes`. This is an adversarial case worth over-documenting; it directly informs
   the evasion-testing work in [THREAT_MODEL.md](THREAT_MODEL.md).

7. **Forwarded / quoted phishing.** A legitimate user forwards a phishing email asking "is
   this real?" The *outer* message is legitimate; the *quoted* content is an attack. Label the
   **authoring intent of the message as sent** → `legitimate`, and note that it contains quoted
   phishing. Do not label the wrapper by its payload.

## Decision Tree

```
START
  │
  ├─ Q1. Is there social-engineering / deceptive intent?
  │      (impersonation, manufactured urgency, credential/payment lure, deceptive link)
  │
  ├─ NO  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────►  legitimate
  │
  └─ YES
       │
       └─ Q2. Which authorship signals dominate the FINAL text?
            │
            ├─ Human tells dominate
            │   (typos, awkward/non-native phrasing, inconsistent register,
            │    formatting seams, flat "straight to the ask")  ──────►  human_phishing
            │
            ├─ AI tells dominate
            │   (uniform fluency, even tone, complete persuasion arc,
            │    flawless institutional register, scaled deep
            │    personalization, no human error artifacts)  ─────────►  ai_phishing
            │
            └─ Mixed / genuinely ambiguous
                 ├─ Label by the dominant signal, AND
                 └─ If still 50/50: escalate to a second labeler;
                    record the conflict and your lean in `notes`.
```

---

## Handling Ambiguous Samples

1. **Re-run the two questions separately.** Most "ambiguity" is really a collapsed
   intent/authorship judgment. Decide intent first in isolation, then authorship.
2. **Weigh the dominant signal, don't average.** Authorship is a lean toward whichever
   set of tells is stronger in the *final* text — not a tally of individual features.
3. **Use metadata when the body is borderline.** The source CSVs retain `sender`,
   `subject`, and `urls`; a spoofed sender or mismatched link can resolve the intent
   question that the body left open.
4. **Escalate rather than guess.** A true 50/50 goes to a second labeler. A coin-flip
   label poisons the training set far more than a deferred one.
5. **Always record reasoning.** Every non-obvious call gets a one-line `notes` entry —
   especially mixed-authorship and evasion cases, which are the most informative for both
   model and threat model.

## Labeler Discipline (summary)

- **Intent first, authorship second.** Never let smoothness or error count override intent.
- **Label the message as sent**, not its quoted payload or its metadata alone.
- **Lean on the dominant signal**; escalate genuine ties.
- **Document every non-obvious call** in `notes`.
- **Over-document adversarial cases** (AI-with-injected-errors, AI-polished human scams) —
  they are the hardest and the most valuable.
